Jim Rogers on Finding Your Passion, Asia's Rise, and Why AI Is Not New

The legendary investor joined Pale Blue Nexus for our very first episode. What followed was a 30-minute conversation about travel, technology, parenting, and the one thing Jim says he has repeated more times than anything else in his life.

By Yohann Calpu

When Pale Blue Nexus launched, there was only one guest Yohann wanted for Episode 1. Jim Rogers had been in his inbox since 2009. A legendary investor who co-founded the Quantum Fund with George Soros, drove a custom yellow Mercedes through 116 countries, and moved his family to Singapore before most Western investors were paying attention to Asia. He said yes.

The conversation covered a lot of ground: macro markets, education costs, the AI cycle, fatherhood at 60, and the yellow car. Here is what Jim actually said, in his own words.

On Asia and Why He Left America

Jim has lived in Singapore for years. He is direct about why.

"Whether we like it or not, Asia is the future. The population is here and the prosperity is here. I don't particularly like it since I'm an American, but America is the largest debtor nation in the history of the world. Asia has lots of prosperity and lots of money. I'm quite happy in Asia."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He raised both daughters to speak Mandarin. His older daughter just graduated from Columbia in New York. His younger daughter is heading to university next year. He is watching the world he predicted come into focus in real time.

On AI: This Has All Happened Before

Yohann asked Jim about the AI wave and whether it was different from past technology cycles. Jim did not hesitate.

"There was a time when the railroads caused great excitement and hysteria and terror. Electricity. Oh my gosh, electricity. There was a time when people were terrified of electricity, telephones, everything. I can remember when computers came along. We were all wildly excited. If you had a company and you had 'computer' in your name on Wall Street, your stock went up. Just because it said computer. Nobody quite knew what you did, but your stock was going straight up. So it's always something new, something exciting. Right now, it's AI. And we're excited about AI. But I repeat, this has all happened before."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He added: "I hope we live long enough to see many many many more new exciting things." His point was not that AI is unimportant. It was that the fear and the hype follow the same script every generation. The people who end up on the right side of these cycles are usually the ones who keep their head while everyone else is losing theirs.

On the Yellow Mercedes and 116 Countries

The car was not an aesthetic choice.

"I specifically got yellow because part of the plan was to drive around the world and I knew that there were very few yellow cars in the world and I knew that they would be distinctive and it might save my life. I knew that wherever I was, people would say, 'Look at that yellow car.' And that means if the bandits were coming, everybody would be watching. So I hoped it would save my life. I'm still alive, so maybe it did."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

The route went from Iceland to Ireland to England, across Russia to China to Japan, back through Siberia to Ireland, then down through 35 countries in Africa, Australia, New Zealand, the tip of South America, and up to Alaska. They slept in the Sahara Desert when there were no hotels. He describes the stars at night there as "glorious."

His conclusion on the trip: "I am lucky to be alive. I'm delighted to be alive."

On Education and Whether an Ivy League Degree Is Worth It

Yohann raised the question a lot of founders are asking right now: is a $100K university education still worth it in the age of AI? Jim went to Yale and Oxford. His answer might surprise people who expect him to say yes by default.

"University is good for people. It helps you grow up. But the best part of a university education is you meet new people, you have new experiences, things that never would have happened to you at home. It's the experiences of meeting new people, having new experiences. That's the best part. You can read all those books wherever you are, whenever you are."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

When Yohann pushed on whether an Ivy League education is meaningfully better than a state school, Jim was candid:

"You can go to the state university in Mississippi or to Princeton. Both will be good experiences for you. It's the experiences you have that are better than anything. Maybe I should have gone to University of Mississippi. It's the experiences and the new life that's the best part of it."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He came from a small town in Alabama where his home phone number was five. "That's not a typo. Five." Going to Yale taught him how to use a dial telephone, among other things. His point: the gap between him and the world closed because of exposure, not curriculum.

The One Thing He Keeps Repeating

If there is a single through-line in everything Jim Rogers says, in his books, his interviews, and this conversation, it is this. He said it multiple times, unprompted, in multiple contexts.

"The main opportunity for everybody is what they love. The most important thing to learn is what you love. And please pursue that. And it doesn't matter what it is. If you want to be a farmer, be a farmer. If that's what you love, you're going to be successful and excited and have a good life. And even if you're not rich and famous, doesn't matter because you'll be happy. And that's the most important."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He ended the episode the same way he has ended countless conversations:

"Please figure out a way to find out what you love the most. And many people never find it, or even if they find it, they're afraid to do it. But the most important thing is to figure out what you love and do it. Pursue it. Do it. Pursue it. Pursue it. Pursue it. It's the most important advice I can give anybody."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He found his thing by accident. He got a summer job on Wall Street because he liked the person who offered it. He fell in love with investing because it paid him to understand what was happening in the world. "Oh my gosh, they pay me. If you do it well, they pay you a lot of money." He had been heading to law school. He did not end up there.

On Becoming a Father at 60

Jim was against having children most of his life. His first daughter arrived when he was 60. His second when he was 65. He did not qualify this.

"All my life I was against having children. I felt sorry for my friends who had children. I had my first child when I was 60 and my second child when I was 65, and I would say it's been astonishing, exciting, wonderful for me. I want to say it's one of the best things I ever did."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

He added that if he had done it at 23, it would have been a mistake for everyone involved. "But there came a time when I was ready. I had done many things." He is now convinced he did it at exactly the right moment for him. He invites you to ask him again in 40 years.

Why Pale Blue Nexus Exists

Yohann explained the premise of the show in his closing remarks to Jim. It is worth quoting directly because it captures what this podcast is actually for:

"My whole concept behind this show is to bring the conversation between humans back to the forefront. A lot of people are talking about tech. A lot of people are talking about AI, and I think in this world we're just trying to make sense of what's happening."

Yohann, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1

Jim's response to that:

"I hope you're going to help all of us learn what's happening and what's going on in the world. We all need to know about the world. We all need to know about reality. The most important thing is to know what's really happening and what it means. Not just what's happening, but what it means. And that's the hard part."

Jim Rogers, Pale Blue Nexus Episode 1
